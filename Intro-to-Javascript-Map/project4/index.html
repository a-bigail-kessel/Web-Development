Diversity in Art Museums

Throughout history, and still to this day, the art world has been dominated by white men and their aesthetic. Due to this, the art most commonly found in popular museums is not reflective of the actual production of art around the world. A group of researchers scoured the public online catalogs of 18 major U.S. museums to truly see the diversity of the bodies of work stored in these museums. Since the data collected for this study relies on inferring ethnicity and gender from what museums provide online, a large portion of the art lacks sufficient enough data to make conclusions on the artists. This data is not included. Moreover, not all works are cataloged online, so the jarring diversity numbers are not 100% reflective of the art at the museum, and instead represent what is easily accessible on the internet. It’s been five years since this study was published, and the study caused a huge movement for more diversity in art, so these numbers have likely changed. Yet, the message they represent is still pressing to this day. I’ve selected five fine arts museums from across the country to display and analyze the ethnicity breakdown of their works. The selected museums are: The Metropolitan Museum of Art, Museum of Fine Arts Boston, Art Institute Chicago, Museum of Fine Arts Houston and Diversity at LACMA.

The Metropolitan Museum of Art

Self-Portrait ca. 1941

Samuel Joseph Brown, Jr.

Diversity at the Met

Out of the five museums, the Metropolitan Museum of Art is about tied with Museum of Fine Arts Houston for the third most diversity, with 11.1% of its works publicly cataloged online created by people of color. The painting displayed on the left by Samuel Joseph Brown Jr. is part of the Met's permanent collection on modern and contemporary art. Brown grew to critical acclaim for his skills as a watercolorist and became the first African American hired by the U.S. government’s first work-relief program for the arts.

Museum of Fine Arts Boston

Cylinder vase. Late Class Period, ca. 600-800 C.E.

Mayan Artist

Diversity at MFA Boston

Out of the five museums, the Museum of Fine Arts Boston has the second most diversity, with 20.1% of its works publicly cataloged online created by people of color. The vase displayed on the left is part of MFA Boston’s exhibit entitled “The Ancient Americas,” which presents work from 36 cultures across South and Central America over two millenia pre-colonization. The vase depicts a gathering of deities who deliver women from an upper world to a lower realm.

Art Institute Chicago

Head (Uhunmwun Elao) ca. 18th/early 19th century

Artist from the Edo Kingdom of Benin

Diversity at AIC

Out of the five museums, the Art Institute Chicago is the least diverse, with 9.6% of its works publicly cataloged online created by people of color. The brass head displayed on the left is part of AIC’s exhibit entitled “Art of Africa,” which displays art from 16th-20th century Africa. The head was commissioned by a newly crowned king for an altar commemorating a previous ruler. The head allows the king to channel his deified ancestors and protect his reign.

Museum of Fine Arts Houston

Pleasure Pillars. 2001

Shahzia Sikander

Diversity at MFA Houston

Out of the five museums, the Museum of Fine Arts Houston is about tied with the Met for the third most diversity, with 11.4% of its works publicly cataloged online created by people of color. The painting displayed on the left is part of a visiting exhibit at MFA Houston called “Shahzia Sikander: Extraordinary Realities” which explores the work of Pakistani-American contemporary artist Shazia Sikander. Sikander is internationally celebrated for combining South Asian manuscript painting traditions with contemporary art practice and modern discussions of femininity.

Los Angeles County Museum of Art

Pure and Hard (Puros y duros). 1991

José Leonilson

Diversity at LACMA

Out of the five museums the Los Angeles County Museum of Art has the most diversity, with 21.8% of its works publicly cataloged online created by people of color. The tapestry displayed on the left was part of a visiting exhibit on Latin-American contemporary art that explored the Latin-American experience over the past 50 years. José Leonilson was a Brazilian artist who experimented with conceptual works focused on love and grief, specifically through a queer lens.
